## QUESTIONS PRESENTED

1. Whether the **Nivens bad-faith exception** to *Younger* abstention applies when a municipal entity (Baltimore Police Department) initiates a retaliatory criminal prosecution in direct response to a citizen obtaining a lawful Peace Order against a third party.

2. Whether municipal liability under *Monell* attaches when a city agency ignores its own documented "pattern or practice" of ADA and First Amendment violations, as identified in a 2016 Department of Justice (DOJ) Investigative Report.

## STATEMENT OF THE CASE

Petitioner was subjected to a retaliatory criminal prosecution (Case No. D-01-CR-25-011146) by the Baltimore Police Department (BPD) after obtaining a lawful Peace Order. The charging documents contained material omissions—specifically the existence of the Peace Order—constituting a *Franks* violation. Although this case resulted in *nolle prosequi* in January 2026, the BPD and the State of Maryland have utilized "strategic mootness" to avoid appellate review of the underlying retaliatory conduct.

The BPD's actions are a direct continuation of the systemic failures documented in the 2016 DOJ Investigative Report, which found a pattern of ADA violations and retaliatory arrests. Despite this "formal notice" of unconstitutional conduct, the BPD failed to provide any ADA accommodations during Petitioner's arrest and subsequent detention, making the municipal entity the "moving force" behind Petitioner's injuries.

---

## REASONS FOR GRANTING THE WRIT

### I. Retaliatory Prosecution as Bad Faith
Under *Phelps v. Hamilton*, 59 F.3d 1058 (10th Cir. 1995), a prosecution brought in bad faith or for purposes of harassment is a recognized exception to *Younger*. The "tit-for-tat" nature of the BPD’s charges, following Petitioner's exercise of his First Amendment right to petition for a Peace Order, meets this high threshold.

### II. Monell Liability and the DOJ Findings
The 2016 DOJ Report established that the BPD was on notice of its systemic constitutional deficiencies. By ignoring these findings and continuing to target disabled citizens with retaliatory prosecutions, the municipal respondents have established a custom and policy of unconstitutional conduct that warrants federal intervention.

## CONCLUSION
The petition for a writ of certiorari should be granted.
