The submission package incorporates the following adjustments from the earlier biotechnology/cybersecurity dual-degree draft and the earlier criminal-justice-only draft:

- **Refocus on the falsifiable core hypothesis.** The dissertation concept has been narrowed from the earlier "biocybernetic" framing to a single falsifiable claim about the SET ledger's *Daubert* admissibility. The neuroprosthetic and closed-loop CRISPR components have been retired.

- **PLA portfolio mapping for the BS degree.** The BS in Criminal Justice is satisfied through a Prior Learning Assessment portfolio, with line-item mapping from the standard BS-CJ learning outcomes to the portfolio artifacts. - **Concrete publication plan with target outlets.** Each of the four dissertation chapters and the two standalone units is mapped to a specific peer-reviewed journal, with a submission target date and a status (existing, in progress, planned). The publication plan supports the portfolio-based PhD model.
- **Honest risk disclosure.** The risk matrix identifies ten specific risks with likelihood, impact, and mitigation. No risk is identified at Critical impact, which is the honest assessment of a tractable but ambitious dissertation.

The package does not include:

- **Letters of recommendation.** The recommender request templates (Enclosure 4 of the materials list, included as `04_recommender_request_templates.md`) are templates the applicant sends to recommenders, not the letters themselves. The letters must come from the recommenders, on their letterhead, and submitted through the program's portal or by email.
- **Official transcripts.** The applicant does not have prior undergraduate coursework that would generate an official transcript. The PLA portfolio is the substitute for the transcript; the institution will specify the form of the PLA documentation required.
- **Standardized test scores.** Most PhD programs in cybersecurity have moved away from GRE requirements. If the target institution requires GRE scores, the applicant should plan accordingly.
- **Application fee.** The application fee is paid through the institution's application portal, not as part of this package.
- **Source artifacts (Enclosure 11).** The HSPA prototype, BSBS whitepaper, ASTA pipeline, and Consolidated SCOTUS Submission are listed as "on request" rather than included in the package, to keep the package length manageable. The applicant should be prepared to provide any of these on request.

The applicant should take the following steps to use this package:

1. **Identify the target institution and program.** The package is written generically for an accelerated integrated BS-CJ/PhD-Cybersecurity program. The applicant should identify the specific target institution and substitute the institution's name in all documents.
2. **Substitute the institution's BS-CJ learning outcomes.** The PLA mapping (Enclosure 5) is written against a composite of three reference programs. The applicant should substitute the actual outcomes of the target institution.
3. **Identify and confirm the recommenders.** The recommender templates (Enclosure 4) are designed to be customized. The applicant should identify the three recommenders, customize the templates, and send them at least 4–6 weeks before the deadline.
4. **Pre-publication review of the writing sample.** The applicant should have the writing sample reviewed by a faculty member or other qualified reader before submission, to ensure that the autoethnographic framing is clear and that the citations are accurate.
5. **Submission through the institution's portal.** Most institutions use an online application portal (e.g., Interfolio, Slate, or a custom system). The applicant should follow the institution's submission instructions exactly.
6. **Pre-publication legal review.** The applicant should have the Writing Sample, the Petition-to-Scholarship Mapping, and the Risk Matrix reviewed by a copyright/privacy specialist (if available through the institution) before submission, to ensure that the inclusion of the SCOTUS petitions and the underlying litigation does not raise any copyright, privacy, or other legal issues.
7. **Be prepared to defend the dissertation concept in an interview.** Most PhD programs in cybersecurity include an interview as part of the admissions process. The applicant should be prepared to defend the falsifiable core hypothesis, the methodology, and the four-year timeline in a 30–60 minute interview.
