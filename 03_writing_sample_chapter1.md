# Writing Sample — Chapter 1, Section 1

## *Quantum-Safe Chain of Custody: A Mixed-Methods Evaluation of Post-Quantum Cryptographic Evidence Integrity in Disability Adjudication and Forensic Science Workflows*

### Chapter 1: The Case File as Data — An Autoethnographic Account of Neurogenetic Evidence Exclusion in Federal Administrative Adjudication

---

#### 1.1 The Procedural History as Empirical Object

This dissertation treats a single, ongoing federal administrative case as a primary source: the consolidated disability adjudication of Caustin Lee McLaughlin, the author, docketed variously as *McLaughlin v. Bisignano* (S. Ct. No. 25-1522) and as the underlying proceedings in the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit (Nos. 25-1222, 25-1224) and the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit. The procedural history of that case, and the manner in which federal agencies and reviewing courts have responded to the proffer of polygenic risk score (PRS) evidence and related neurogenetic biomarkers, is the dissertation's empirical anchor. It is offered here not as advocacy — the underlying litigation remains pending and the author is the named party — but as a documented instance of a recurring structural phenomenon: the categorical exclusion of cryptographically anchored biometric and neurogenetic evidence from federal administrative adjudication.

The use of an autoethnographic case file as data carries well-known risks. The author is not a detached observer; he is the affected claimant. The findings generated from the case are not generalizable in the statistical sense; they are analytically generalizable in the sense articulated by Robert K. Yin and by Sarah J. Tracy, who describe qualitative case study as a vehicle for "theoretical generalization" rather than "statistical generalization." The methodological commitments that follow from this choice are addressed in §1.4. For present purposes, it is sufficient to note that the dissertation does not depend on the case for its empirical weight. Chapter 2 is a systematic review. Chapter 3 is a network simulation campaign. Chapter 4 is a Delphi panel evaluation. The case file serves only as the framing device that motivates those empirical studies and as one observation against which their results are triangulated.

#### 1.2 The Biographical Predicate

The author is a neurodivergent adult with a documented polygenic risk profile in loci empirically associated with executive dysfunction, including the catechol-O-methyltransferase gene (*COMT* rs4680, val158met), the dopamine receptor D4 gene (*DRD4* 48-bp VNTR), the FK506-binding protein 5 gene (*FKBP5* rs1360780), and the sodium voltage-gated channel alpha subunit 2 gene (*SCN2A*). The polygenic profile is not, in itself, a diagnosis. It is a probabilistic input to a multi-factor clinical assessment, comparable in evidentiary weight to a single laboratory value within a broader diagnostic panel.

The author has, over the period 2022–2026, sought federal disability benefits through the Social Security Administration (SSA) and ancillary accommodations through the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) and the Department of Justice (DOJ). Across multiple administrative law judge hearings, two consistent patterns have emerged. **First**, administrative law judges have declined to admit the polygenic evidence on the ground that PRS is "not a recognized medical diagnostic" — a categorization that, while doctrinally defensible under the SSA's Listing of Impairments (20 C.F.R. Pt. 404, Subpt. P, App. 1), elides the distinction between diagnostic and probabilistic evidence, a distinction the Federal Rules of Evidence have not historically required. **Second**, the underlying administrative record has been preserved using a mixture of legacy cryptographic primitives (SHA-256, RSA-2048) that are projected to be vulnerable to cryptographically relevant quantum computers within the data's expected confidentiality lifetime — a "harvest-now, decrypt-later" exposure that the SSA's own 2024 Cybersecurity Assessment implicitly acknowledges but does not remediate for claimant records.

The procedural pattern, in other words, is not a single agency's error. It is a structural interaction between (a) evidentiary doctrine that has not been updated to accommodate probabilistic genomic evidence, (b) administrative procedure that treats cryptographic chain-of-custody as a security matter rather than as an evidentiary matter, and (c) the cryptographic migration timetable of the federal government, which has prioritized citizen-facing authentication (Login.gov, ID.me) over the long-confidentiality claimant record.

#### 1.3 The Certiorari Posture

Petition 1 in the consolidated Supreme Court submission, *McLaughlin v. Bisignano* (No. 25-1522), asks the Court to resolve three questions:

> **Question 1.** Whether the categorical exclusion of objective genetic and neuroimaging evidence — specifically Polygenic Risk Scores (PRS), validated neurobiological correlates (e.g., *COMT* rs4680, *FKBP5* rs1360780), and neuroimaging biomarkers of executive dysfunction — by federal agencies (the Social Security Administration and the Department of Health and Human Services) in disability determinations constitutes arbitrary and capricious agency action in violation of the Administrative Procedure Act (APA), 5 U.S.C. § 706, and the Due Process Clause of the Fifth Amendment, when such evidence is scientifically validated, clinically relevant, and directly probative of the claimant's functional capacity.
>
> **Question 2.** Whether the federal courts, including the Court of Federal Claims and the Federal Circuit, commit reversible error by failing to exercise their inherent authority to grant incidental equitable relief — including but not limited to WCAG 2.2-compliant interfaces, Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) security for protected genetic data, and AI/LLM-assisted cognitive interfaces — necessary to ensure meaningful access to justice for neurodivergent litigants in Tucker Act claims under 28 U.S.C. § 1491, thereby creating a jurisdictional trap that denies any forum for redress and violates the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) Title II and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act.
>
> **Question 3.** Whether the "ping-pong" procedural history of this case — characterized by the ultra vires transfer of a Tucker Act claim from the Court of Federal Claims to the Federal Circuit, the dismissal of complaints while in forma pauperis motions were pending, and the systematic refusal to adjudicate claims involving neurogenetic evidence — constitutes a structural denial of access to justice that warrants Supreme Court intervention under the All Writs Act, 28 U.S.C. § 1651(a), and the Court's supervisory authority.

The petition is not the dissertation. It is an advocacy document and is offered here only as documentary evidence of the procedural facts that Chapter 1 reports. The dissertation's argument is not that the Supreme Court should grant certiorari; that is a question of Supreme Court practice and discretion, and one on which reasonable jurists will differ. The dissertation's argument is the narrower and more tractable claim that the architectural pattern described in Chapter 3 — the Hybrid Semantic Processing Architecture with the Semantic Evidence Trace ledger — supplies the kind of cryptographic chain-of-custody infrastructure that *Daubert* doctrine has, in other contexts, treated as a relevant indicator of evidentiary reliability. *See* *Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc.*, 509 U.S. 579, 593–94 (1993) (identifying "known or potential rate of error" and "the existence and maintenance of standards controlling the technique's operation" as relevant indicia).

#### 1.4 Methodological Commitments

The use of a first-person case file as data requires explicit methodological disclosure. The dissertation adopts the following commitments, drawn from the autoethnographic and qualitative case study literature:

**First**, the case file is treated as a *bounded system* (Yin, 2018, ch. 2) and not as a universal generalization. The case is generalizable to the class of federal administrative adjudications in which (a) the claimant has documented neurogenetic risk markers, (b) the administrative law judge has authority to exclude evidence under the SSA's Listing of Impairments framework, and (c) the underlying administrative record is preserved using cryptographic primitives projected to fail within the record's confidentiality lifetime. The class is not small; the SSA processes approximately 2.5 million disability claims annually, of which a non-trivial minority involve documented neurogenetic markers. The class is also not universal; the case cannot speak to state administrative adjudication, private disability insurance, or non-U.S. systems.

**Second**, the case file is offered under a *reflexive disclosure* commitment. The author's status as the named party is disclosed at every empirical use. The author has not edited the case file to remove adverse procedural facts. Adverse procedural facts — including the dismissal of the complaint in the Court of Federal Claims while the in forma pauperis motion was pending — are included in the case file and are discussed in §1.5.

**Third**, the dissertation is subject to the standard *member-checking* and *audit-trail* commitments of qualitative research. The transcripts and exhibits underlying the case file are public court records (PACER) and are available for independent verification. The Chapter 4 Delphi panel is composed of credentialed domain experts who are not the author and who evaluate the SET ledger output against the *Daubert* prongs without knowledge of the author's litigation posture.

#### 1.5 A Single Documented Instance: The "Ping-Pong" Problem

The procedural posture of the underlying litigation is, on its own, informative. The author filed a complaint in the U.S. Court of Federal Claims in late 2024, asserting a Tucker Act claim under 28 U.S.C. § 1491 for the SSA's failure to provide cryptographically adequate protection of the claimant's protected health information. The case was transferred *sua sponte* to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit on the theory that the claim sounded in tort rather than in contract. The Federal Circuit dismissed the transferred complaint while the author's motion to proceed in forma pauperis was still pending, foreclosing the in forma pauperis pathway and effectively terminating the case at the circuit level. The author then filed a parallel action in the U.S. District Court for the District of Maryland, which was dismissed on *Rooker–Coleman* grounds. The author has filed a petition for a writ of certiorari in the Supreme Court, presenting the questions quoted in §1.3.

The "ping-pong" terminology used in Question 3 is the author's. It is descriptive of the procedural fact pattern: the same substantive claim has been transferred between three federal forums in approximately eighteen months, with each forum declining jurisdiction on grounds that depend on the characterizations made by the prior forum. The pattern is not, in the author's view, a defect of any single judicial officer. It is a structural feature of a system in which the substantive claim — that a federal agency's cryptographic infrastructure does not meet the constitutional minimum for the protection of biometric and neurogenetic data — does not have a clearly designated forum.

That structural observation is the dissertation's point of departure. The substantive claim may be granted or denied on the merits; reasonable jurists will differ. The structural claim — that the claim has no designated forum — is empirically observable from the PACER docket and is not, on the author's reading, in serious dispute.

#### 1.6 What the Case File Does Not Show

The case file does not, and cannot, show that PRS evidence ought to be admitted in SSA disability adjudication on the merits. The merits question is a question for the SSA, the reviewing courts, and ultimately the Supreme Court; the author has views, but those views are not the dissertation's argument. The case file shows, at most, that the doctrinal infrastructure for considering PRS evidence is unsettled, and that the cryptographic infrastructure for the underlying record is obsolete on the agency's own migration timetable. Whether the doctrinal and cryptographic gaps ought to be closed, and if so how, is the question the dissertation turns to in Chapters 2 through 4.

#### 1.7 Chapter Overview

The remainder of the dissertation proceeds in four chapters.

**Chapter 2** is a PRISMA-style systematic review of post-quantum cryptographic implementations in healthcare environments, covering the period 2019–2026. The review identifies the empirical latency and availability benchmarks that have been published, the regulatory frameworks that have been invoked, and the gaps that remain. The review is the dissertation's literature foundation.

**Chapter 3** is the architectural and simulation analysis. It specifies the Hybrid Semantic Processing Architecture (HSPA-9), the Semantic Evidence Trace (SET) ledger, and the SHA3-256 / ML-KEM-768 / ML-DSA-III integration. It reports the results of a Mininet/GNS3 simulation campaign measuring latency, throughput, and packet loss under simulated DDoS and HNDL attack scenarios. The simulation campaign is the dissertation's original technical contribution.

**Chapter 4** is the *Daubert* validation study. It reports the results of a structured Delphi panel of administrative law judges, federal magistrate judges, and forensic science practitioners evaluating the SET-anchored evidence packages against the four *Daubert* prongs. The Delphi study is the dissertation's original legal-empirical contribution.

A brief conclusion integrates the chapters, identifies the dissertation's contribution to the cybersecurity, evidence law, and criminal justice literatures, and proposes the regulatory pathway by which the SET ledger output might, in subsequent work, be admitted under the Federal Rules of Evidence.
